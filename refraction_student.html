Refraction of Light

Year 8 Physics  |  Building on: The Law of Reflection

1. What is Refraction?

You already know that when light hits a mirror, it bounces back — that's reflection. But what happens when light passes through a material instead of bouncing off it?

When light travels from one material into another (for example, from air into water), it changes speed. If it hits the boundary at an angle, this change in speed causes the light to change direction. This bending of light is called refraction.

The Rules of Refraction

  1. Light bends towards the normal when going into a denser material (e.g. air → glass or water)
  2. Light bends away from the normal when going into a less dense material (e.g. glass → air)
  3. If light hits the boundary straight on (0°), it does not bend at all
  4. The angle of incidence and angle of refraction are both measured from the normal, not the surface

2. Reflection vs Refraction — What's the Difference?

Feature Reflection Refraction
What happens to light? Bounces back Passes through and bends
Does it cross the boundary? No Yes
Does speed change? No Yes
Example Mirror, still water surface Glass, water, lenses

3. Refraction in the Real World

Refraction explains many everyday observations. Here are some you should be able to explain:

Observation Caused by refraction? Brief explanation
A straw looks bent in a glass of water ✓ Yes Light bends as it leaves the water, making the straw appear to shift
Your reflection in a mirror ✗ No This is reflection, not refraction
A rainbow ✓ Yes Water droplets refract sunlight and split it into colours
A swimming pool looks shallower than it is ✓ Yes Light bends as it leaves the water, making the bottom appear higher
A magnifying glass making things bigger ✓ Yes The curved lens refracts light to converge rays and create a larger image
A shadow on a wall ✗ No Shadows are caused by light travelling in straight lines, not refraction

4. Snell's Law (The Maths)

Scientists use a formula to calculate exactly how much light bends. It's called Snell's Law:

n₁ sin θ₁  =  n₂ sin θ₂
SymbolWhat it means
n₁Refractive index of material 1 (where light starts)
n₂Refractive index of material 2 (where light goes)
θ₁Angle of incidence (measured from the normal)
θ₂Angle of refraction (measured from the normal)

Refractive index (n) tells us how much a material slows light down. Air ≈ 1.0, water ≈ 1.33, glass ≈ 1.5. A higher number means light slows down more.

Worked Example

  1. Light travels from air (n₁ = 1.0) into glass (n₂ = 1.5)
  2. The angle of incidence is 30°
  3. Using Snell's Law: 1.0 × sin(30°) = 1.5 × sin(θ₂)
  4. sin(30°) = 0.5, so: 0.5 = 1.5 × sin(θ₂)
  5. sin(θ₂) = 0.5 ÷ 1.5 = 0.333
  6. θ₂ = sin⁻¹(0.333) = 19.5°
